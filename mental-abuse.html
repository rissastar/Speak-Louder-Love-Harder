Emotional Abuse

Just because you can’t see the wounds, doesn’t mean they’re not real.

What Is Emotional Abuse?

Emotional or psychological abuse involves patterns of behavior that harm a person's emotional well-being or sense of self-worth. It can include manipulation, gaslighting, threats, constant criticism, isolation, and other controlling behaviors.

Unlike physical abuse, emotional abuse can be subtle and long-lasting—often making survivors question whether what they’re experiencing is “bad enough” to count. But it is. Abuse is abuse.

Signs of Emotional Abuse

  • Being constantly blamed, shamed, or criticized
  • Being isolated from friends or family
  • Walking on eggshells to avoid conflict
  • Feeling "crazy" because your experiences are invalidated
  • Being called names or insulted
  • Having your feelings used against you
  • Being manipulated through guilt, fear, or affection

How to Get Help

Emotional abuse is serious—and healing is possible. If you're in an emotionally abusive situation, you deserve support and protection.

  • Talk to a trusted therapist or counselor — You are not overreacting.
  • Reach out to a support line:
    • Canada: 1-866-863-0511 or text 540-540 (Talk4Healing)
    • USA: 1-800-799-SAFE (7233) — National Domestic Violence Hotline
    • Chat online with The Hotline

Emotional abuse doesn’t always leave visible scars — but it leaves lasting damage. It reshapes how you see yourself, how you trust others, and how safe you feel in your own skin.

From Family
It started early. My own family taught me to hide my emotions, to be the strong one, to stay quiet even when I was hurting. When I expressed pain, I was “too sensitive.” When I had needs, I was “too much.” I was made to feel like a burden — like I had to earn love, and even then, it could be taken away at any moment.

From Bullies
School wasn’t safe either. I was picked apart by bullies who saw my differences and used them as ammo. They mocked my health, my past, my appearance — anything they could. Those voices followed me long after school ended. And the worst part? Eventually, I started believing them.

From Relationships
In my relationships, the abuse wore a different mask. It looked like love at first. Then it looked like control, jealousy, manipulation. I was told what I could wear. I was kept from friends. I was gaslit until I questioned my own reality. I was made to feel crazy for needing support.

Some of them sexualized me in humiliating ways — accusing me of things I hadn’t done, twisting innocent moments. I felt violated without ever being touched. I lost my sense of safety in my own body.

But I’m Still Here
Emotional abuse doesn’t end when the person walks away. It echoes. It sticks. But so does healing. I’ve had to unlearn everything I was taught about love and worth. I’ve had to relearn how to trust myself, how to speak up, and how to believe that I deserve more.

If you’re in it — if you’ve been hurt by family, bullied in school, or broken down in a relationship — I want you to know:
It’s not your fault. You are not “too much.” You are enough. And what happened to you was abuse, even if no one ever hit you.

You deserve to be safe. You deserve to be heard. And you deserve to heal on your own terms.
